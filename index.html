Module 02 / 04
Digital-enabled Ways of Working

How You Work With Digital

Two short workplace situations. For each, choose the response that most honestly reflects what you would actually do — not the ideal answer.

Situation A — Your team · A recurring workflow

Your team handles a recurring process that involves multiple manual steps — compiling inputs from different sources, formatting them, and routing them for review and approval. It works, but it is slow and error-prone. A digital tool is available that could automate several of these steps. You have been asked to look into it. What do you do?

A
Review the tool independently and, if it looks suitable, direct the team to switch over and phase out the manual process.
B
Map the existing process with the team first to understand where the real friction points are, then assess whether the tool actually addresses them before deciding whether to adopt it.
C
Flag it to the team as something worth exploring, and invite whoever is interested to trial it on a smaller task and report back.
D
Check whether other teams have already adopted it and what their experience has been before committing to any change.
E
Step back and look at whether the process itself needs simplifying first. If the manual steps can be reduced or eliminated through better workflow design, that may be the right fix — with or without a digital tool.
Situation B — Cross-divisional project · Mid-delivery

You are coordinating a piece of work involving colleagues from three other divisions. Progress is slower than expected. Updates are coming in through a mix of emails, chat messages, and informal check-ins, making it hard to track where things stand. A team member suggests moving everything onto a shared digital workspace so the whole team can see status in one place. What do you do?

A
Agree it is a good idea and set up the shared workspace yourself, then brief the group on how to use it.
B
Support the suggestion but raise it with all division leads first — getting buy-in before changing how the group works will matter more than the tool choice itself.
C
Trial the shared workspace with your own division's members first to work out the setup before rolling it out to the wider group.
D
Ask the team member who suggested it to propose a specific tool and a simple protocol, then bring that to the group for a decision together.
E
Keep the current approach for now and focus on tightening the check-in rhythm instead — a clearer meeting cadence and a shared email thread may be sufficient without introducing a new platform mid-delivery.

Digital Leadership Mindset

Your Leadership Stance

Six pairs of statements. For each pair, choose the one that more honestly reflects how you lead today. Both options are held by capable leaders — this is about understanding where you currently are, not where you should be.

PAIR 01 OF 06 — On experimentation
I'd rather my team master a proven process and execute it reliably.
or
I'd rather my team experiment with improving the process, even if it produces uneven results along the way.
PAIR 02 OF 06 — On role-modelling digital adoption
I tend to let my team find their own way with new digital tools — they often know more about the tools than I do.
or
I make a point of visibly using new digital tools myself, so my team can see that adoption is expected at all levels.
PAIR 03 OF 06 — On addressing resistance
When my team is resistant to a new digital initiative, my first instinct is to communicate the rationale more clearly.
or
When my team is resistant to a new digital initiative, my first instinct is to understand what specifically is making them hesitate.
PAIR 04 OF 06 — On communicating digital change
When communicating a digital initiative to stakeholders, I focus primarily on the benefits it will deliver.
or
When communicating a digital initiative to stakeholders, I address both what will improve and what will need to change or be given up.
PAIR 05 OF 06 — On building a digital-ready team culture
Building digital capability in my team is primarily a matter of ensuring they have access to the right training and tools.
or
Building digital capability in my team requires me to actively shape the culture — creating space for experimentation, tolerating early mistakes, and recognising effort as much as results.
PAIR 06 OF 06 — On creating conditions for digital development
If a team member wants to develop their digital skills, the motivation and initiative should primarily come from them.
or
As a leader, I see it as my responsibility to actively create opportunities for my team to develop and apply digital skills in their work.
