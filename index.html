Module 02 / 04
Digital-enabled Ways of Working

How You Work With Digital

Two short workplace situations. For each, choose the response that most honestly reflects your first instinct — not the ideal answer. You may feel you would do more than one of these; that is entirely normal. Choose the option that reflects your strongest inclination.

Situation A — Your team · A recurring workflow

Your team handles a recurring process that involves multiple manual steps — compiling inputs from different sources, formatting them, and routing them for review and approval. It works, but it is slow and error-prone. A digital tool is available that could automate several of these steps. You have been asked to look into it. What do you do?

A
Assess the tool myself and direct the team to adopt it if suitable.
B
Map our current process with the team first, then assess whether the tool actually addresses our friction points.
C
Flag it to the team and invite interested members to trial it on a smaller task.
D
Check how other teams have found it before committing to any change.
E
Step back and simplify the process first — the right fix may not require a digital tool at all.
Situation B — Cross-divisional project · Mid-delivery

You are coordinating a piece of work involving colleagues from three other divisions. Progress is slower than expected. Updates are coming in through a mix of emails, chat messages, and informal check-ins, making it hard to track where things stand. A team member suggests moving everything onto a shared digital workspace so the whole team can see status in one place. What do you do?

A
Support the idea and set up the shared workspace myself, then brief the group.
B
Raise it with all division leads first — buy-in matters more than the tool choice.
C
Keep the current approach and tighten the check-in rhythm instead — email and a clearer meeting cadence may be sufficient.
D
Trial the workspace with my own team first before rolling it out to the wider group.
E
Ask the team member who suggested it to propose a specific tool and protocol, then decide together as a group.

Digital Leadership Mindset

Your Leadership Stance

Six pairs of statements. For each pair, both options are held by capable leaders — choose the one closer to your default instinct, even if both feel partly true.

PAIR 01 OF 06 — On experimentation
I prefer my team to master a proven process and execute it reliably.
or
I prefer my team to experiment and improve our processes, even if results are uneven at first.
PAIR 02 OF 06 — On role-modelling digital adoption
I let my team find their own way with new digital tools — they often know more than I do.
or
I visibly use new digital tools myself, so my team sees that adoption is expected at all levels.
PAIR 03 OF 06 — On addressing resistance
When my team resists a digital initiative, I focus on communicating the rationale more clearly.
or
When my team resists a digital initiative, I focus on understanding what is specifically making them hesitate.
PAIR 04 OF 06 — On communicating digital change
I focus on the benefits when communicating a digital initiative to stakeholders.
or
I address both the benefits and what will need to change when communicating a digital initiative to stakeholders.
PAIR 05 OF 06 — On building a digital-ready team culture
I build digital capability in my team by ensuring they have access to the right training and tools.
or
I build digital capability in my team by actively shaping the culture — making space for experimentation and tolerating early mistakes.
PAIR 06 OF 06 — On creating conditions for growth
I expect team members who want to develop their digital skills to take the initiative themselves.
or
I actively create opportunities for my team to develop and apply digital skills in their work.
